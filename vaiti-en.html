Designed from scratch the interfaces and logic for a career service by bringing together
job search, job listings, a company directory, an interview trainer
and tools into one unified workspace. Created a shared design system, which connected the services and prepared the product for a fast launch and future growth.
About the Project
Vaiti is a job-tech startup that helps people find jobs in IT. Instead of
using many different websites, the product offers a single platform. It brings together
job search, a company database, skill development, and the tools needed to apply.
My Role
When I joined Vaiti, the team did not yet have a dedicated designer. The product was being built
collectively, so the interface lacked a unified logic and end-to-end user flows.
There were already some early drafts, but many of them had to be completely rethought.
My main task was to turn business ideas into clear, usable interfaces.
I reviewed questionable decisions, rebuilt screen logic, and looked for a balance between business goals
and user convenience.
What I Did
I took full ownership of the product design: discarded some of the existing drafts,
introduced new decisions with clear reasoning, and designed all key scenarios and screens.
Throughout the process, I built the interaction logic across the services and laid the foundation
for an interface system so the product would feel coherent and predictable for users.
Research
Constraints
The project was at the MVP stage, so we could not stay in research for long: the team
had to move to interface design quickly and push the product into development.
Competitors
I ran a quick analysis of direct and indirect competitors. For direct competitors, I studied job-tech
services — how their job listings, filters, cards, and company pages
were structured. For indirect competitors, I looked at products for interview preparation, resume building,
and skill development.
For each service, I documented its strengths and weaknesses. I looked for patterns
users were already familiar with, and noted where the interface was overloaded or made the
flow harder.
Users
It was easy for me to understand the target audience because shortly before the project, I had been looking
for a job in IT myself. From my own experience, I knew how hard it is to compare job requirements, choose
companies, and prepare for interviews.
During the work, I also ran quick hallway tests with developer friends
who matched the product’s target audience. I showed them individual
flows, asked questions about search, filters, job cards, and applications, and watched
where the logic was not read as intended.
Job Aggregator
This was the first module I used to shape the entire product. This is where
the overall interface logic emerged and the UI kit began to take shape.
Job search is always stressful. Users have to compare
dozens of roles, salaries, tech stacks, and company reliability at the same time. I wanted
the Vaiti interface to feel like a calm, clean space that does not pressure
the user. Soft gradients helped make the screens feel visually lighter, keeping the focus
on the text and the substance of the jobs.
Key Decisions
One Job per Row
Initially, I designed the results in two columns because I wanted to show as many
options as possible on one screen. But after studying research and articles on user behavior
in such interfaces, I dropped the idea. It became clear that during job search,
people value calm, sequential reading more than content density.
Two columns created extra visual noise in a context where maximum focus is needed.
Relevant Jobs Instead of Filters
Initially, users had to configure filters manually. Later, we added
a resume-based job matching feature: the algorithm suggests relevant options automatically. I brought
the smart matching toggle to the foreground and hid the classic filters behind a button.

This decluttered the interface and made search faster.
Shortlist and Compromise
The business wanted to make the shortlist the only path to applying: liked a job →
saved it → opened the shortlist → launched auto-apply. This flow
was already complex, but the main issue was that auto-apply did not work everywhere, only with an integration
with the platform or through a special plugin. For most users, this would have become a dead end.
I suggested making the “apply at source” button the primary action. This way,
we could guarantee that the user could always complete the target action. I kept the shortlist
as a secondary flow — for those who want to collect options first and then apply
to them in one go. In the end, we agreed on this solution.
Job Page
The business wanted all information to expand directly inside the general list on click.
I insisted on creating a separate page. Without it, the listing would have turned
into overloaded chaos that would be hard for users to navigate.
On the page itself, I placed the full job description and made “Apply
at source” the main action, while keeping auto-apply secondary. I also added
a block with the company’s rating and contacts. This lets the user immediately evaluate
both the job and the employer without switching between tabs.
Swipe Mode
I designed an alternative browsing mode that does not exist on the Russian market. Instead of
a list, there are cards: swipe left to skip, swipe right to add to the shortlist.
The card opens in an expanded state right away. It contains only the essentials
for a quick decision: employer, role, salary, tech stack, key task, and an
auto-apply marker. The full description can be read below by scrolling.
I made the switch between list and cards clearly visible so users could easily
change the browsing format on the fly. I also added a counter nearby that shows in real time
how many jobs were found in total and how many are left to view with the selected filters.
Outcome
This section became a full-fledged workspace for job seekers. I managed
to bring a complex search process into one clear system, where all the tools for working
with jobs are gathered in one place.
Company Directory
There was no company directory in the product — only a ,
grouped by category, with no descriptions, search, or context. I suggested
reworking this block and turning it into a full product section with convenient
cards, search, and filters.
Key Decisions
Two Columns
Unlike the job list, here I intentionally used a two-column grid. Companies
are searched for differently: users usually do not read the details closely, but scan the page looking for
familiar brands or making a quick comparison. Two columns speed up this process
without overloading the screen.
Company Card
Inside the card, I surfaced only what matters most for an initial assessment: business area,
short description, tech stack, and two ratings — overall rating and hiring difficulty.
Here, users can also immediately view contacts for recruiters or the company itself.
Targeted Search and Filters
I added search by name and filters by key parameters: technologies, company size,
and field of work. This turned a simple catalog into a convenient tool
for targeted employer search.
Reviews
The collection form is designed so users can share details about their
interview process. It includes the number of meetings, whether there was a test task, discussion topics
with management, and even specific questions or tasks they were given during the interview.
Outcome
Right now, the directory covers the MVP’s core tasks: finding a company, getting a quick overview, and accessing contacts. But I designed this interface from the start so it can be easily scaled later and expanded with full company pages.
Competency Directory
This section solves the uncertainty problem for job seekers who often do not understand their
real level or market expectations. Here, users simply choose their specialization
and immediately get a complete, clear map of the profession’s requirements.
Key Decisions
Grades
To help the team easily fill this section with content, I designed a flexible structure
of blocks. Through the admin panel, different forms and data formats can be combined
for the specifics of any IT profession.
Salary
The data is hidden — users get access after sharing their own salary.
The database is filled with real numbers from real people.
Related Technologies
Users can study each technology in more detail without leaving the section,

which helps them understand the ecosystem of the chosen field faster.
Technical Question Database
I gathered all preparation questions in one place and organized them by topic and grade.
Users can filter them by their level and prepare calmly
for technical interviews. They no longer need to search for question lists across the internet; the entire
process now happens inside the platform.
Outcome
This section fully covers the user’s main need — to understand market requirements
and determine their level. At the MVP stage, it works as a convenient reference
and self-assessment tool. Later, the competency map can be turned
into a personal navigator that recommends topics and materials for moving
to the next grade.
Interview Preparation
This section helps users train their technical knowledge before a real interview. At the
MVP stage, the trainer is implemented, while online testing and a sandbox are planned as the next stages
of development.
Key Decisions
Recent Sessions
I brought the history of recent practice sessions to the section’s main screen. This lets the user
instantly continue an interrupted session or quickly review a completed topic without losing
their progress.
Trainer Setup
I split preparation into three simple steps: choosing a specialization, technology, and grade.
The user sequentially selects their direction, the required tech stack, and the desired
grade. This removes the feeling of complex configuration.
Results
I designed the results screen so users can immediately review their mistakes.
The system clearly highlights which answers were correct and which were not. This turns the test
from a simple score into real help.
Outcome
This section helps job seekers assess their readiness before entering the market. For now,
only the interview trainer is live in the product, but the interface is already ready for new features.
Most importantly, users now have a convenient starting point: they can launch new
practice sessions, track their percentage of correct answers, and see all their progress in one
place.
Cover Letter
This section removes routine from the job application process. The interface’s main task is
to help job seekers create a text in just a couple of clicks that perfectly connects their experience
with the requirements of a specific job.
Key Decisions
Source Flexibility
At the MVP stage, the tool can collect data only from the Vaiti directory. But I immediately
designed the logic so the system would not be tied to a single source.
If a user finds a job on another website, they can simply copy
its description and paste it manually. This makes the letter generator useful in absolutely any
scenario.
Focus on the Final Action
I designed the final screen so users can immediately get the result
of their work. The copy and download buttons are placed in the most visible spot.
This lets users take the finished letter in one click.
Outcome
I managed to launch a useful tool that reduced writing
a cover letter to just a couple of clicks. We worked around the limitations of the first product version
through flexible data input. Users can now quickly take a ready-made
letter structure and immediately adapt it to an employer’s requirements.
Resume Improvement
This section helps job seekers find mistakes in their profiles and fix them.
It helps improve a resume so it can successfully pass
screening bots and appeal to real HR professionals. At the same time, the interface is designed so
users do not lose control over the content and always decide for themselves which edits
to accept and which not to.
Key Decisions
Change Control
I immediately rejected the idea of applying all edits blindly with one button. Instead,
the system clearly organizes the AI’s suggestions into categories: it separately checks
structure, achievements, and compatibility with ATS filters. Each suggestion
shows the text in a before-and-after format. This lets users decide for themselves which
wordings work for them and which should remain unchanged.
On-the-Spot Editing
So users would not have to download the document and edit it manually, I added
an editor directly inside the suggestion card. If the AI-generated version seems
not quite accurate, users can quickly adjust it manually before finally accepting
the changes.
Sticky Panel
There can be many suggestions and edits in a resume. To keep users from getting confused
or losing their changes while scrolling, I added a floating panel at the bottom of the screen.
It always stays visible. There, the user sees the exact number of selected improvements
and can apply them all at once in one click.
Recruiter-Approved Template
The preview window shows the finished PDF document. The resume template itself was designed
not just for visual aesthetics — I aligned its structure with an active
recruiter. This ensures that the final file is generated exactly in the format
employers expect to see. From the same window, the resume can be downloaded or saved in one click
to the platform profile.
Outcome
This section turned working with AI from blind generation into convenient collaborative
editing. I managed to create a tool that carefully highlights
a user’s growth areas while leaving the final decision to them. As a result, the job seeker
gets a professionally structured document ready to send to employers.
UI Kit
The project’s design system was built with a focus on development speed and ease of maintenance.
I did not just assemble a component library; I adapted it to the real needs
of front-end development to prevent misunderstandings during implementation.
Base Styles and States
I immediately documented all rules: from brand gradients to spacing and typography.
For each element, I designed a clear state grid. The developer did not have to
guess how a button looks on hover or when disabled — all these scenarios
were prepared in advance, protecting the interface from visual clutter.
Future-Ready Components
I organized all elements in Figma so the product could be easily developed further.
When the team decides to add new sections in the future, they will not have to redraw
basic patterns. The design will remain consistent, and new screens will be assembled
from ready-made building blocks.
Outcome
I managed to turn a broad idea into a clear structure and interfaces while maintaining a balance
between business goals and value for candidates. I created the logic and rules by which
the product can continue to evolve.
The project is currently in development. I fully prepared and handed over to the team all
materials — from user flows to the adaptive UI Kit, creating a solid foundation for fast
implementation.
